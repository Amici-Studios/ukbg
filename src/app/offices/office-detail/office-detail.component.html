In the event of a death

How long to register a death in CITY_NAME

In the event of a death the law states the death must be registered within 5 days, unless an inquest has been called then it will be at the discretion of the coroner. The reporting of the death must be made by a relative, a person present at the time of death or the person who’s dwelling/site where the body was discovered. When a death occurs it must be registered at the District Register Office of the town/city it took place.

When registering the death to the registrar you will be asked questions regarding the deceased family etc. Check all answers given before you sign the register to prevent mistakes being made. You will be given a Green Form which grants the permission of the funeral and a form for Social Security purposes. Additional copies of certificates can be provided upon request at a set fee. If the coroner is involved there will be a different set of procedures, they will inform you of which at the time.

Unexpected Death

When a death occurs in a hospital, the chosen next of kin of the deceased will be contacted by hospital staff. The deceased body will be kept in the hospital mortuary until the executors make the necessary arrangements, their possessions will be kept securely until the next of kin can collect them. If you wish you can ask to see the Hospital Chaplain.

If you discover a body you should contact:-

The Police, who will also help to find the relatives etc
If known, the Deceased’s Relatives
If known the Deceased’s Family Doctor

If there is a reason to suspect that death was not from a natural cause, you must ensure not to touch or remove anything from the deceased or the surrounding areas. The death may be referred to the coroner for a post-mortem examination, if agreed to by the next of kin, then this will provide the cause of death. In some cases, this will mean that the funeral cannot take place until authorised by the coroner.

In the case of an expected death, you should contact the doctor of the deceased, so he/she can certify the cause of death and he/she will be able to give you the necessary documents:

Professional Counselling

Bereavement counselling is a specialised type of counselling that involves supporting individuals who have experienced the loss of a loved one. This counselling helps them work through their grief as well as perhaps learn coping mechanisms to help them when they are on their own. Bereavement counselling is recommended for anyone, of any age, whose loss seems overwhelming or whose life is being adversely affected by their grief.

Turning to bereavement counselling after the loss of a loved one is not an admission of weakness, but instead, it is an admission of the strength to seek help when it is needed. Whether it be one-on-one with a private therapist or in a group setting, counselling aims to help an individual explore his or her emotions. At the first meeting, the bereaved will likely be asked about his or her loss, about his or her relationship to the deceased, and about his or her own life now that person has lost a loved one. Answering these questions often means tapping into sadness or anger, so emotional outbursts should not be censored. Crying and yelling may come naturally during bereavement counselling and certainly will not offend the counsellor.

Call a professional today if you or somebody you know will benefit from the help of a counsellor.
